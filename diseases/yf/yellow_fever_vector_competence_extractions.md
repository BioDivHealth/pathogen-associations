Extraction notes
- Broad field survey from southeastern Brazil; partial support only, not a complete yellow fever vector list.
- `Haemagogus janthinomys` and `Haemagogus leucocelaenus` are treated as competent because the paper explicitly calls them the primary vectors and ties them to high abundance plus natural infection.
- `Sabethes chloropterus`, `Aedes scapularis`, and `Aedes taeniorhynchus` are kept conservative because the paper frames them as local, secondary, or first-detection roles rather than clearly established competence.
- `Aedes aegypti` and `Aedes albopictus` are negative rows because the paper says there was no evidence of YFV transmission by either species.
- This PDF appears to be a duplicate copy of the same underlying paper as the next Abreu section; the rows are repeated here only because the user asked for one section per PDF.

## Damasceno-Caldeira et al. - 2023 - Vector Competence of Aedes albopictus for Yellow Fever Virus Risk of Reemergence of Urban Yellow Fe.pdf

```csv

```

Extraction notes
- Narrow laboratory competence study focused on one mosquito species; not a complete yellow fever vector list.
- The row is `mixed` because transmission occurred at 21 dpi, but the same experiment did not show transmission at 14 dpi.
- Saliva positivity plus transmission to the non-human primate model make this a strong competence row, but it is still condition-dependent.

## Gabiane et al. - 2024 - Evaluating vector competence for Yellow fever in the Caribbean.pdf

```csv

```

Extraction notes
- Narrow single-species laboratory study; not a complete yellow fever vector list.
- `Mixed` is the right label because all tested populations could transmit, but transmission success varied by mosquito population and YFV genotype.
- The paper gives strong saliva/transmission evidence, but not a uniform competence result across all populations.
